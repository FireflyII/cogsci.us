Game / Experimental Design Document

Purpose

In order to find/demonstrate that problems of different kinds are handled differently, an environment will be created that can be manipulated in specific ways that alter the available knowledge, and therefore the type of problem, while recording a subject’s actions. The hypothesis is that altering one or more of these factors will not only make the task more or less difficult, but be a significant enough change that an agent will take different approaches to it.

General Design

The four components to be manipulated are different kinds of information the subject will have about their task, which will be in the form of a computer game. These components are “Clear Possibilities,” “Clear Course,” “Clear Progress,” and “Clear Goal-Reaching.” Applied to a game, this amounts to whether the player knows A.) What they can do, B.) What to do next, C.) Whether they are advancing towards their goal, and D.) If they have successfully completed their goal. A game environment was chosen as it seems to lend itself most easily to varying these levels of information without dramatically altering the structure of the task itself. There are a few different game styles that may work well, and one in particular that seems flexible enough to lend itself to a study of this kind.

Roguelike Games

The type of game to be used is a top-down, two dimensional world commonly called a roguelike, or dungeon crawler. The player is represented as a character on the screen, and has simple controls for moving around in an enclosed area with a number of paths to take. Often there are doors that block the way until an item is found or an enemy defeated. Messages or clues can be found, equipment such as tools or clothing can be gained and used, and other characters and creatures may share the environment. There are a wide variety in games of this type, but some common features include:

  • Limited visibility of the world at any given time
  • Unexplored areas are completely hidden until visited
  • “Fog of war” hides any activity occurring outside the player’s immediate area, even though the map is visible
  • An inventory of items can be carried, as well as a limited number of slots for “equippable” items
  • Health, stamina, magic reserves, and other player attributes may be affected by the environment
  • Progress blocked until some condition is met, usually requiring going back and re-exploring a previous area
  • Multiple directions to explore, some with useful items, some with dangerous elements, some with nothing at the end, and one (or more) that will advance the game.
  • Different terrain (mud, water, sand, stone, ice, etc.) that may change how quickly movement can be made, or restrict passage selectively
  • Internal “puzzles,” such as arranging objects in a specific order, making the correct sequence of moves from one room to another in a maze, pushing obstacles out of the way without blocking movement, etc.

Manipulations for a Roguelike

There are a number of possible ways to take a specific roguelike game and adapt it so that different kinds of information are or are not available to the player. Here are some ideas for each of the problem features.

  • Possibilities
  • Clear possibilities (clear what you can do globally in the game):
  • The player could be given instructions on how to move and interact, including explaining keys/doors, buttons, etc.
  • Unclear possibilities (unclear what you can do):
  • The player is given no (or incomplete) instructions about how to move and control the world, leaving them options that they are unaware of, such as fake walls, vertical movement, doors that appear to have something behind them that won’t be openable, etc
  • Course
  • Clear course (clear what you should do at a given moment in the game):
  • The player can see far enough ahead to know which direction to go next, or which should be used first (such as knowing that an item from one room will be required in another, so that item should be found first).
  • Unclear course (unclear what you should do):
  • The player has multiple directions they can take, and no visual clues to guide them regarding which path to take.
  • The field of view can be restricted so that the player isn’t fully aware of their environment, and whether they should go back the way they came, or proceed forward
  • Progress
  • Clear progress:
  • A progress bar, or numerical counter may indicate how much a task has been completed, or how close the player is to an objective.
  • A mini-map may included so that the player can see how much they have explored, compared to how much remains to be seen.
  • A point of interest may be indicated on the mini-map, so that the player can tell if they are getting closer or farther from it.
  • Rooms can be visually distinct in order to aid in wayfinding.
  • Unclear progress:
  • No record of how many objectives have been done versus how many remain.
  • No map to orient where the player is within the world.
  • Unconventional room movement (i.e. a maze that loops back on itself) to make it unclear if the player is advancing at all.
  • Visually similar rooms make it difficult to track the player’s progress
  • Goal-Reaching
  • Clear goal reaching:
  • The game positively alerts the player that they have successfully accomplished a goal.
  • The player has a clearly defined task that they can tell has been completed, such as “find 5 coins” or “unlock and pass through this door.”
  • Unclear goal reaching:
  • The game does not alert the player when a task has been accomplished, leaving it up to the player to decide when they are done. The game may further question the player in order to promote uncertainty.
  • The player has a task such as “find all coins” without knowing how many there are, and therefore providing uncertainty as to whether they have all been found.

Dependent Variables

Within the framework of this game engine (Phaser/Javascript), a number of features can be continuously recorded.  Primary variables to be captured include:

  • The player’s position within the game and on the screen at all times, as well as which direction it is facing.
  • Time and duration of any button presses (as well as the time without action being taken, and which buttons are pressed).
  • When and where the mouse is clicked and/or dragged, as well as when is moved, and where, and whether it is inside or outside the window of the game (and when it enters and leaves).

Also available are:

  • When the player intercepts other objects/items in the game, such as coming into contact with walls, different floor areas, moving objects, etc.
  • Status changes, such as open or closed hands, objects being held or carried, etc.
  • The actual size of the world on the player’s screen (meaning whether it’s in a small window or a large one, suggesting large it is visually to the player).
  • How long it takes for each task to be completed, and all movement of the player between them.

Screenshots 

The following are side-by-side examples of ways in which each of the four criteria may be manipulated in order to control how much information a player has when attempting to play a game.

Clear / Unclear Possibilities

Clear Possibilities

While the player’s view of the world is restricted, there are visual cues to indicate ways they can and cannot move. More explicitly, the player is given clear instructions about their environment and what they can do, providing them with clear possibilities when choosing how to act.

Unclear Possibilities

In direct contrast to the case with clear possibilities, the player in this instance is given no formal guidance, and therefore doesn’t know how to move, where they can go, or what to expect. Visual cues like walls may suggest possibilities, but deviations from those expectations, such as finding that some walls can move, or that not all floor tiles are safe, can further undermine the player’s ability to know all of their possibilities.

Clear / Unclear Course

Clear Course

Coming from the upper part of the screen, the player comes to a fork the road, and needs to decide which path to take. In the left image, the course is clear, since one path is open, while the other has a dangerous lava pit in the way!

Unclear Course

On the right, the player can only see where they’ve already been, so both paths appear equally viable, and the player doesn’t have anything to indicate which direction to go, constituting an unclear course.

Clear / Unclear Progress

Clear Progress

The player can be provided with an unambiguous way to determine how much of a task they done, compared to how much remains, by employing the use of a simple progress bar. The game’s instructions would explain what it means to the player in order for them to be able to clearly gauge their progress.

Unclear Progress

Without an obvious record of how close they are to achieving their goal, the player is left uncertain regarding their progress. The measure of progress could be any of a number of things, from exploring a sufficient amount of the map, to collecting a number of items, to finding a particular location, but without some indicator to aid the player there is insufficient information for them to accurately judge how much progress they have made.


Clear / Unclear Goal-Reaching

In all instances, the player will be told at the beginning of the game what their goal will be, so they will always know what they’re meant to be striving for. The contrast between clear and unclear goal-reaching is based on whether they player has a way to tell when that goal has been met.

Clear Goal-Reaching

To end the game, the player needs to hit a button. In the image on the left, as the player approaches the button they are told that they have successfully completed their task, making it clear that the goal has been reached.

Unclear Goal-Reaching

In the image on the right, the player is told that only that the nearby button will end the game. They are given no indication about whether or not they have met their intended goal, and are left to decide for themselves as what point they think they’re done. In this version of the game, the player will be told at the start not only what their goal is, but whether or not they will be informed when it has been completed. This method is used in the crowd-sourced game Eyewire, in which users must identify all of the branches of a neuron from one end of a sliced image to another, without selecting parts of other neurons. Eyewire provides no feedback to the user (after the tutorial levels) with which to judge progress or completion, only offering a numerical score when the user decides they’re done.
